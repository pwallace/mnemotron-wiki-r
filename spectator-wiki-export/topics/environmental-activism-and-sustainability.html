Environmental Activism and Sustainability at Hamilton

Overview

Hamilton College’s organized environmental movement began in the late 1980s with the founding of the Hamilton Environmental Action Club (soon renamed the Hamilton Environmental Action Group, or HEAG), which became the college’s primary vehicle for student environmental advocacy across four decades. From early campaigns around Earth Day 1990 and the county-mandated recycling program, through food-waste audits and composting proposals in the late 1990s, to Green Week programming, the Recyclemania competition, and LEED-certified construction in the 2000s, to the college’s signing of the American College and University Presidents’ Climate Commitment in 2007, HEAG’s advocacy on bottled water and fossil fuel divestment in the 2010s, and the Student Assembly Sustainability Committee’s formation in 2019, the environmental movement at Hamilton reflects both persistent student organizing and gradual institutional responsiveness. As of 2019, Hamilton had articulated a carbon-neutrality goal of 2050 under a Climate Action Plan but had not yet hired a dedicated sustainability director, placing it among only a handful of colleges without one.

Key Points

Pre-HEAG environmental activism (early 1970s). Environmental organizing predates HEAG by at least two decades. In April 1972 the Spectator reported on the Environmental Ecology committee of Hamilton (referred to by the abbreviation “EECHK”), which urged students to write state legislators about several environmental bills then before Albany, including the Environmental Impact Statement bill (A9245a) and measures governing nuclear power plant siting. This shows an early tradition of student lobbying on environmental legislation that HEAG would later continue. (The Spectator, April 28, 1972)

HEAG’s founding and early activism (1989–1990). By fall 1989 a student environmental organization was operating under the name “Hamilton Environmental Action Club.” Beth Fuller ‘90, writing in the October 27, 1989 Spectator, described the club’s collaboration with the national Student Environmental Action Coalition (S.E.A.C.) to encourage students to write Congress in support of the Global Warming Prevention Act. The club was already framing climate change in terms of fossil-fuel combustion and deforestation, concerns that would define HEAG’s agenda for the next thirty years. (The Spectator, October 27, 1989)

Earth Day 1990 and recycling program origins. The twentieth anniversary of Earth Day in April 1990 was a pivotal public moment for Hamilton environmentalism. The Environmental Action Club organized tree planting, an “Earth Day Pledge” signature drive, film screenings, and tabling on deforestation, recycling, and ozone depletion on Minor Field. Coverage documented tension with other campus groups who felt environmental programming competed with the “Al-Ham Day Cookout”; a Spectator editorial urged students to “Look Past the Hill on Earth Day.” Concurrently, a campus recycling program moved from feasibility into operation: recyclables were sorted into bins for paper and aluminum, with proceeds directed toward program costs. The program soon became mandatory under an Oneida County recycling ordinance, requiring both the college and its residential students to comply. An April 1990 lecture by journalist Michael Brown, who broke the Love Canal story, linked environmental toxins to public health, reinforcing a broad ecological frame. (The Spectator, April 20, 1990; The Spectator, April 27, 1990; The Spectator, April 6, 1990)

HEAG in the early-to-mid 1990s. Through 1991–1995, HEAG (by then using the “Action Group” name) maintained weekly meetings and appeared regularly in campus activity calendars. The group advocated for energy conservation within the Physical Plant, monitored lamp installations, and co-sponsored Earth Day events with the Campus Activities Board. A notable 1990 speaker series brought Stephan Schwartzman of the Environmental Defense Fund to campus to lecture on Amazon deforestation and the murder of rubber-tapper leader Chico Mendes, connecting environmental destruction to social justice — a framing that would recur in later campaigns. By the mid-1990s HEAG’s focus included pesticide controls, ozone policy, and expanded recycling, and the group was building the institutional relationships with Physical Plant and dining services that would define its most productive campaigns. (The Spectator, November 2, 1990; The Spectator, April 14, 1995)

HEAG’s peak organizational capacity and Albany lobbying (1996). By spring 1996 HEAG had developed a multi-front Earth Day program that combined campus petitioning with state-level lobbying. On April 22, 1996, three HEAG members (Maria Gara ‘98, Chris Snow ‘98, Jeff Evans ‘99) attended EarthFest ‘96 at Mohawk Valley Community College, while five others traveled to Albany to lobby legislators on five bills: establishment of a Maine Woods National Park, opposition to Governor Pataki’s plan to log the Allegheny State Forest, a Pepsi boycott over investments in Burma, Superfund expansion, and a composting bill. The group also collected more than 800 signatures from Hamilton students and staff requesting the elimination of fluorescent paper on campus. Professor of Environmental Studies Bill Pfitsch was quoted: “Everyday is Earth Day.” (The Spectator, April 26, 1996)

Food-waste audit and composting proposals (1997–1998). The most concrete institutional campaign of the late 1990s was HEAG’s 1997 food-waste measurement project at Commons and McEwen dining halls. Jeff Evans ‘99, the group’s coordinator, led the effort: on October 27 and 29, 1997, students collected separate receptacles of leftover food waste, measuring 251 pounds from Commons on Monday and 242 pounds from McEwen on Wednesday — a total of 493 pounds over two days, extrapolating to an estimated 34,510 pounds per semester. Bon Appétit’s general manager Joe Cappa cooperated with the audit, establishing an institutionalized channel for environmental proposals to dining services. Evans proposed composting, reducing pre-made sandwiches, and limiting paper cups. By late 1997, HEAG was also planning a campus-wide audit of paper, plastic, cleaning solvents, energy, and water use. The Keehn Co-op was already running its own composting system. Hamilton did pilot a post-consumer organic composting program in 1998 and 1999, though it was discontinued before being reinstated. (The Spectator, November 7, 1997; The Spectator, November 21, 1997)

Recycling performance and “Captain Planet” (2002). An April 2002 Spectator piece published in honor of Earth Day framed Hamilton’s recycling performance critically: the college recycled only 14% of its waste, compared to a national average of 30% and Middlebury College’s nearly 60%. Physical Plant had recently introduced a three-bin system (Container, Paper, Garbage) with clear bags to enable sorting, and HEAG members — listed as “Captain Planet’s Helpers” — were named as contacts for questions. The piece highlighted the structural challenge of getting students to properly sort recyclables before collection. (The Spectator, April 19, 2002)

Green Week and HEAG’s programmatic expansion (2004–2009). By 2004 HEAG had institutionalized an annual Green Week, a five-day October program covering food awareness, energy efficiency, and recycling. The 2004 edition included a screening of Super Size Me (with discussion on the environmental impact of the American diet), a Campaign for Clean Energy, and distribution of recycling travel mugs in partnership with the Physical Plant. HEAG had approximately fifty members and operated under a co-presidency model — typically three presidents. The 2006 Green Week featured a “Trash Mountain” installation, a visible pile of one day’s campus waste, to dramatize actual waste production. HEAG’s Jen Kleindienst ‘09 also chaired the Recycling Task Force, a student group operating under Physical Plant supervision that tracked Hamilton’s performance in the national Recyclemania competition. Physical Plant confirmed that the college had piloted composting in 1998–1999 but that space constraints related to KJ construction delayed reinstatement. (The Spectator, October 22, 2004; The Spectator, October 27, 2006)

Sustainability report card and LEED certification (2007). By spring 2007, HEAG was presenting annual external sustainability assessments of Hamilton’s environmental performance across categories including Climate Change and Energy, Green Building, and Food and Recycling. Bon Appétit’s practices earned Hamilton an “A” in food and recycling; the renovation of Skenandoa Hall earned LEED certification, contributing to a “B” in Climate Change and Energy categories. HEAG advocated for publication of these assessments beyond internal use. That same year, President Joan Hinde Stewart signed the American College and University Presidents’ Climate Commitment (ACUPCC), committing Hamilton to a twofold mission of carbon neutrality and institutional sustainability leadership. The college set an initial target of 20% carbon-footprint reduction by 2015 and a zero-emissions goal for 2050. (The Spectator, March 9, 2007; The Spectator, September 28, 2017)

SA defunding, Building Dashboard, and solar panels (2006–2008). In a significant setback in November 2006, Student Assembly approved zero dollars for HEAG’s budget, directing the group to seek funding from the Environmental Studies Department or the President’s Office instead. Despite this, HEAG remained active. By fall 2008, Hamilton had launched a Building Dashboard — a real-time, web-based energy monitoring system — which HEAG co-president Jeremy Gleason planned to use for a dorm energy competition. Solar panels were being installed on the Kirner-Johnson building and Outdoor Leadership Center (Glen House), and a windmill was operating on the south side of campus; however, in September 2008 eight solar panels worth over $8,000 were stolen from the Glen House, delaying completion of that installation. (The Spectator, November 3, 2006; The Spectator, September 19, 2008; The Spectator, October 10, 2008)

Dorm energy competitions, Skenandoa House, and composting momentum (2009). HEAG’s co-president Nat Duncan ‘12 described the group’s 2009–2010 priorities: monitoring building heat in winter, sponsoring dorm energy competitions (the 2008 competition had reduced energy use by 17% and saved approximately $14,000), working with ITS on eco-friendly technology, and pursuing campus-wide composting. The newly built Skenandoa House represented the college’s most advanced green building to that point: its geothermal heating system used 250% less energy per square foot than a comparable natural-gas system, and its use of renewable electricity reduced CO₂ emissions by 107.1 tons in its first year. Physical Plant’s Steve Bellona confirmed a composting program was “in the works for October” 2009. (The Spectator, September 10, 2009; The Spectator, September 17, 2009)

Hydrofracking protests and Powershift (2011). HEAG expanded its advocacy from campus to regional policy in spring 2011. Will Gowen ‘11 marched with approximately 500 protesters in Albany against New York State’s practice of hydraulic fracturing for natural gas, citing risks to the water supply of roughly nine million state residents. That same weekend HEAG sent a larger contingent to the national Powershift 2011 conference in Washington, D.C., focused on protesting environmentally damaging energy production. HEAG’s biannual Farmer’s Market and Green Week (featuring a Pledge to Go Veg event, a “Lights Out, Green In” speaker, and a campus glen cleanup) continued as the group’s domestic programming anchor. (The Spectator, April 14, 2011; The Spectator, April 26, 2012)

HEAG and the fossil fuel divestment movement (2013–2015). By fall 2013 HEAG’s Spectator column was linking its work explicitly to the national fossil fuel divestment campaign, noting that “the divestment campaign has spread to over 300 colleges and universities across the country.” In September 2014, HEAG and the Fossil Fuel Divestment Organization co-organized Hamilton’s participation in the People’s Climate March in New York City, joining approximately 400,000 marchers. HEAG President Risa Nagel ‘16 described the march as a “hopeful scene.” In September 2015, HEAG co-hosted a campus divestment debate alongside Hamilton Divests. The two organizations remained institutionally distinct — HEAG focusing on campus environmental practices, the divestment group on endowment policy — but collaborated on climate-related programming. (The Spectator, October 10, 2013; The Spectator, September 25, 2014; The Spectator, September 24, 2015)

“Take Back the Tap,” SA resolution, and carbon-neutrality pledge (2016). In fall 2016 HEAG achieved one of its most concrete recent policy wins: Student Assembly unanimously passed a resolution — initially drafted by HEAG President Jack Wright ‘19 — to reduce Hamilton’s consumption of single-serving water bottles from over 41,100 per year to 4,000 by 2020. The campaign, called “Take Back the Tap,” was grounded in evidence that Hamilton purchased more than 41,100 single-use bottles from Bon Appétit annually. The 2016 academic year also saw the college confirm its carbon-neutrality goal: President Joan Hinde Stewart had signed the ACUPCC in 2007 committing Hamilton to carbon neutrality, and by 2016 two major solar power projects and a wind turbine were contributing over 40,000 kilowatt-hours of renewable energy annually. Steve Bellona, Associate Vice President for Facilities and Planning, was the primary administrator driving the carbon-neutrality strategy. (The Spectator, September 29, 2016; The Spectator, October 6, 2016; The Spectator, March 30, 2017)

Carbon neutrality by 2050 and the sustainability director gap (2017–2019). A March 2017 Spectator feature on Bellona detailed Hamilton’s carbon-neutrality roadmap: a 2050 target covering all college-related emissions from heating and electricity to waste disposal and study-abroad travel. The college’s Climate Action Plan was managed through a Sustainability Working Group led by Brian Hansen, Director of Environmental Protection and Safety, with subcommittees on energy management, education, and natural resources. In fall 2017 a Spectator column noted that Hamilton had signed the ACUPCC in 2007 and compared its progress unfavorably to peers: Middlebury achieved carbon neutrality in 2016 and Colby in 2013. A 2019 feature on the newly formed SA Sustainability Committee directly identified a critical institutional gap: Hamilton was “one of maybe three other colleges that do not have a sustainability director,” despite having a Climate Action Plan and a 2050 carbon-neutral commitment. Jay Carhart ‘21, a Sustainability Coordinator, proposed building green roofs on KTSA and KJ and maintaining Glen trails. (The Spectator, March 30, 2017; The Spectator, September 28, 2017; The Spectator, October 24, 2019)

Cafe Opus composting, Opus sustainability overhaul, and HEAG’s “Take Back the Tap” (2017). In March 2017 Cafe Opus management launched a sustainability overhaul that introduced composting using corn-based biodegradable cups, bowls, and utensils. Opus began composting all food scraps at the local Crane Farm on College Hill Road following HEAG’s “Take Back the Tap” campaign. McEwen added composting bins for recyclable and trash streams. The Spectator noted that students struggled to understand proper disposal of materials, and that Opus’ initiative was an attempt to address both waste volume and environmental education. (The Spectator, March 30, 2017)

SA Sustainability Committee and 2019 climate organizing. In spring 2019 Student Assembly established a Sustainability Committee in collaboration with Hamilton Sustainability Coordinators, with Amanda Kim ‘21, Gianni Hill ‘21, and Mike Moubarak ‘21 as key figures. The committee organized monthly meetings with all sustainability clubs — including HEAG, Hamilton Aquaponics, and Slow Food — and planned a monthly climate newsletter and community composting program. During Fallcoming 2019, student representatives (including members of the Climate Strike and Sunrise Movement) presented Hamilton’s sustainability goals to the trustees, pressing for fossil fuel divestment and deeper climate action. President Wippman’s representatives reportedly “placed a lot of emphasis on students to do better,” which students found an inadequate response. HEAG remained listed as an active student organization in the 2019 student activities roster. (The Spectator, October 24, 2019)

Open Questions

  1. When exactly was the “Environmental Action Club” formally organized — was there a founding year, constitution, or institutional recognition before 1989? Is there earlier documentation in the Spectator (pre-1988)?
  2. Did HEAG ever succeed in establishing a permanent campus-wide composting program after Evans’ 1997–1998 proposals and the short-lived 1998–1999 pilot? What ended the pilot, and when (if ever) was it reinstated?
  3. Who held HEAG leadership after Jeff Evans ‘99 graduated (1999–2004)? Are there issues documenting that transition and any continuity of organizational strategy?
  4. Did Hamilton ever appoint a dedicated Sustainability Director or Coordinator as a permanent administrative position, and if so, when? The 2019 coverage identified this gap as a significant institutional weakness.
  5. What is the current (post-2020) status of the carbon-neutrality-by-2050 goal and the Climate Action Plan? Has it been updated or accelerated following pandemic-related emissions changes?
  6. Was the fossil fuel divestment request ever granted by the Board of Trustees? The coverage through 2019 shows consistent student advocacy but consistent trustee resistance.
  7. What happened to the “Take Back the Tap” goal of reducing single-use bottles from 41,100 to 4,000 by 2020? Was it achieved or measured?
  8. How did HEAG’s structure, membership, and activities change after 2019 (pandemic era and beyond)? Are there post-2020 Spectator issues that document continuity or disruption?
