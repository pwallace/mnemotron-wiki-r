Campus Computing and Technology

Note: This era-specific stub has been superseded by Computing and Technology at Hamilton.

Overview

The period from 1996 to 2003 at Hamilton College was one of rapid and transformative change in campus computing infrastructure. Starting from a small email-connected Science building in late 1992, Hamilton achieved campus-wide ethernet networking by 1995–1996, gained broad internet access, and by 1997 was deploying a Remote Collaboration Facility for video-based teaching. Late 1998 brought Y2K preparedness planning, and the early 2000s saw wireless and laptop adoption begin to change classroom expectations. Information Technology Services (ITS) — led in this period by Director David Smallen — was the administrative unit responsible for all of these transitions.

Key Points

Campus network rollout (1992–1996): Campus-wide networking began in fall 1994 under Jim Huang, an ITS staff member who arrived in summer 1992. The Science building was the first connected to the email system in late 1992 or early 1993. By February 1996 the network was largely complete, with Huang’s team having deployed ethernet cards and network software across the campus. A Spectator profile described Huang as “the man behind the campus network” and documented the speed of the rollout: when the first batch of networked computers arrived, ITS staff “loaded them with ethernet cards and network software lickety-split” and prepared for thirty or forty student support calls that afternoon. (The Spectator, February 9, 1996)

ITS organization: ITS employed approximately 35 student Service Assistants and provided support to “all members of the Hamilton College community in their use of computers, networks and telephones.” ITS advertised actively for student Service Assistants and positioned the work as a way to “develop technical and interpersonal skills.” (The Spectator, February 23, 1996)

David Smallen, Director of ITS: Smallen served as Director of ITS from at least 1997 onward. He communicated with the campus community via email on network issues (documented October 1997) and was quoted extensively in Y2K coverage. Computer Science professor Mark Bailey also commented on technical aspects of Y2K in the same 1998 article. (The Spectator, October 24, 1997; The Spectator, November 20, 1998)

Remote Collaboration Facility (1997): A $75,000 Alden Trust grant equipped Hamilton’s Remote Collaboration Facility (RCF) with laptop computers in 1997. The RCF was designed to support electronic seminars with students and faculty at other institutions, allow remote teaching across multiple locations, and enable videoteleconferences with guest experts. President Tobin described it as enabling entire classes to “go on-line simultaneously.” (The Spectator, April 11, 1997)

Mellon Foundation project (1997): ITS was involved in a Mellon Foundation-funded educational technology project in fall 1997, cited by Smallen in connection with campus computing expansion. (The Spectator, November 14, 1997)

Y2K planning (1998–1999): The November 1998 Spectator ran a detailed article on Hamilton’s Y2K preparedness. Smallen explained that “some systems will fail, or applications will continue to function but give wrong and misleading information.” ITS maintained a dedicated website with Y2K information and links. Professor Mark Bailey noted that “the only solution to the problem is to re-write the software and rebuild the hardware.” Hamilton expected most systems to be compliant by January 2000. The article surveyed the national landscape of federal agency Y2K grades (from the Social Security Administration’s A+ to the Department of Transportation’s F). (The Spectator, November 20, 1998)

Laptop adoption discussion (1998–2000): By fall 1998, other colleges were beginning to require first-year students to purchase laptops. A November 1998 Spectator article surveyed this trend — noting Northern Michigan University’s push to have all students on network-compatible laptops by 2001 — while Hamilton had not yet adopted such requirements. A fall 1996 article noted two laptops stolen from Fuqua science building, reflecting the presence of portable computers on campus even in the earlier period. (The Spectator, November 20, 1998; The Spectator, November 22, 1996)
