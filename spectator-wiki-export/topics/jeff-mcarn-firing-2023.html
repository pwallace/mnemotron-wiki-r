Rev. Jeff McArn Firing (June 2023)

Overview

In June 2023, Hamilton’s head chaplain Rev. Jeff McArn was fired after 27 years of service, without any confirmed malfeasance made public. The dismissal prompted one of the largest unified cross-community responses documented in the 2014–2025 corpus: the faculty voted 110–8 expressing confidence in McArn, and 37 campus organizations signed a letter of support. McArn partially returned as Senior Lecturer in spring 2024, but the chaplaincy position remained effectively unstaffed through at least March 2025.

Key Points

  • McArn’s tenure: Rev. Jeff McArn served as Hamilton’s head chaplain for 27 years before his June 2023 firing
  • Reason: No confirmed malfeasance was established or made public as the basis for the dismissal
  • Faculty response: The faculty voted 110–8 expressing confidence in McArn — one of the most lopsided faculty confidence votes documented in the extended corpus, and a significant faculty-administration rupture
  • Organizational response: 37 campus organizations signed a letter of support for McArn — one of the largest unified cross-community responses documented in the 2014–2025 corpus, drawing together diverse student, faculty, and staff organizations
  • McArn’s partial return: McArn partially returned to campus as Senior Lecturer in spring 2024, though not in his chaplaincy role
  • Chaplaincy vacancy: The chaplaincy position remained effectively unstaffed following McArn’s departure
  • Search delay: A national search for a Dean of Spiritual and Religious Life was not launched until March 2025 — nearly two years after the firing
  • Wippman era context: The firing occurred under President Wippman’s administration; it was among the most significant faculty-administration ruptures of the 2016–2024 period
  • Concurrent crisis: The McArn firing occurred in the same year as the April 16, 2023 Jodel shooting threat, placing two major community crises in the same academic year
  • Institutional significance: The 110–8 faculty vote and 37-organization letter represent an unusual degree of unified institutional opposition to an administrative decision, making the McArn case a landmark in faculty governance and community advocacy
