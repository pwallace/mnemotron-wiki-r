Early Computing on Campus (1981–1987)

Note: This era-specific stub has been superseded by Computing and Technology at Hamilton.

Overview

Hamilton’s computing infrastructure expanded substantially through the mid-1980s, moving from shared terminals connected to a Cornell mainframe to a dedicated on-campus Computer Science Center stocked with Apple Macintosh personal computers. The arrival of eighteen Macintoshes in fall 1985 marked a turning point, enabling word processing and programming by the general student body, not just computer science students.

Key Points

Pre-1985 Infrastructure

Before the Macintosh era, Hamilton’s computing consisted mainly of Terak terminals connected to a main computer at Cornell University, supplemented by a small number of personal computers. The Computer Center was sited in the basement of Burke Library. By 1982 the Computer Center was already pressing for more classroom space, and reports noted the Audio-Visual department’s planned move to the James Library would free additional square footage for computing (Spectator, February 12, 1982). The 1983 Curriculum Commission (chaired by Professor Robin Kinnel, Chemistry) expressed pleasure with “the growth of computer literacy among students” but hoped for more (Spectator, April 8, 1983).

Arrival of Macintoshes, 1985

In fall 1985 Hamilton purchased eighteen Apple Macintosh personal computers for the Computer Science Center in Burke Library. Anne Ludington, head of the Math and Computer Science Department, said the primary purpose was instruction, particularly for introductory courses CS 141 (Macintosh Pascal) and CS 242. She also planned to offer a computer science concentration “within a couple of years.” Academic Computer Coordinator Frank Price predicted that word processing would be the Macs’ dominant campus use: “most of what we do here at Hamilton is process words.” Price described them as “for the first time … a computer that is powerful enough and versatile enough for everyone to use” (Spectator, September 13, 1985).

Growth of Computer Science

A standalone computer room was constructed in the Biology Department around 1985, reflecting computing’s spread into the sciences. By 1985 the Math and Computer Science Department was working toward a formal computer science concentration. By 1987, Apple Macintosh computers (and the bundled Microsoft Works suite) were advertised directly to students in the Spectator, signaling that personal computer ownership was becoming a mainstream student expectation (Spectator, February 13, 1987).
