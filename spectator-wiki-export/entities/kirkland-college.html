Kirkland College

Overview

Kirkland College was a coordinate women’s liberal arts college founded in 1968 adjacent to Hamilton College in Clinton, New York. It operated as an independent institution sharing some facilities and administrative functions with Hamilton until the two merged in 1978, with the merged institution retaining the Hamilton name.

Relevance to Research

Kirkland appears in the corpus beginning with the 1968–69 academic year and becomes an increasingly central subject through the merger in 1978 and into the early post-merger years. The Spectator integrated Kirkland editors and reporters from the fall 1969 issue onward. Coverage spans Kirkland’s founding rationale, its developing campus and programs, the relationship between the two student bodies, and the merger debate.

Notes

President: Samuel F. Babbitt led Kirkland in its first years; he delivered remarks at the second Kirkland convocation in September 1969.

Board: Walter Beinecke Jr. served as Chairman of the Hamilton Board of Trustees and spoke at Kirkland’s 1969 convocation.

Founding rationale: Hamilton chose the coordinate model rather than direct coeducation, described by VP Paul D. Carter ‘56 as “a very daring move.” President McEwen is credited with initiating the planning.

Campus: Adjacent to Hamilton. Had its own dormitories (including one called Kirkland Dormitory), offices, and developing facilities. A new library, health center, auditorium, and other shared facilities were planned as of 1970.

Traditions: Bagpipes at convocation, established in Kirkland’s first year (1968).

Merger (1978): The merger ended Kirkland’s independent existence. Kirkland’s campus, some programs, and faculty were absorbed into Hamilton. The Kirkland name was eventually preserved through the Kirkland Project and related institutional memory efforts.

Distinguishing pedagogy: Kirkland was known for an innovative, student-centered approach to liberal arts education that contrasted with Hamilton’s more traditional curriculum. This distinction is likely documented in Spectator coverage of both institutions.
